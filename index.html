Abstract


This Article presents the description of Quantum radar technology, which is based on the consideration of the principle of Quantum Entanglement. The development of Quantum radar system can be important in many avenues of civil and military environments, as it can obtain information about an object at the distance using a cluster of entangled photons to produce a high-resolution image of that object. Nevertheless, a brief history and current limitations of classical radars were outlined to demonstrate the advantages of Quantum radar system over its counterpart. As well as the advantages, technical difficulties and engineering challenges were mentioned to indicate that it is not the easiest task to complete, as establishment of complementary systems is necessary to make a Quantum radar work.


Introduction


Quantum Radar is a subset of Quantum Sensing Technology, which itself can be used in a wide variety of detecting and scanning systems (future.weapons,2021). When QS applied to radars, it significantly improves its detecting, identification and reconnaissance capabilities (future.weapons,2021). Interest in Quantum Radars has risen from investors and government since 2020 when early results demonstrated an advantage of the QR over its counterpart (see Figure 1:MckinseyDigital).

History


Originally, the first radar as we all know it today had been invented by the British Physicist Robert Watson Watt in 1930(future.weapons,2021). The British Air Ministry asked him to explore the nature of radio waves and how they can be weaponised against enemy bombers (future.weapons,2021). While doing his studies he managed to find out that radio waves used by the BBC broadcasting were reflected from a metal plate (future.weapons,2021). Hence, he discovered that the reflected signal can be used for identification and detection of the incoming enemy planes (future.weapons,2021). Straight after that the system of such radio emitting stations was created in the east and south coast of Britain (future.weapons,2021). The maximum range of one radio emitting station at that time was around 190km (future.weapons,2021).


Limitations of Classical Radars


Since the days of Robert Watson many years have passed, and the radars have been modernised and equipped with much more advanced sensors, which allowed to increase the range of radar to approximately 400 miles (future.weapons,2021). However, till this day their detecting capabilities are still limited (future.weapons,2021). One of the limitations is the inability to detect stealth aircrafts (future.weapons,2021). To avoid radar detection, they are covered in special coating, which allows to absorb electromagnetic radiation rather than reflect it (future.weapons,2021). Even then, any detection would not tell you it’s exact location and altitude(future.weapons,2021).


Next step in radar technologies


The further evolution in radar technologies can overcome the limitations of classical radars by replacing the use of EM waves with quantum states of photons (MIT Technology Review, 2019). The radars, which are using this principle called quantum radars. QR will be able to detect and identify altitude, geolocation and provide a detailed image even of a stealth aircraft, while remaining completely untraceable for modern radio detecting systems, as photons are used instead of waves(IEEEXplore, 2017). There are two major types of QR are highlighted (Mathewes, M. 2010): 1.Un-entangled single photon radar 2. Entangled-Photon Quantum Radar


Un-entangled single photon radar


Single – Photon QR in nature is very similar to a classical radar (Irving, M. 2020). The transmitter generates a single photon that is fired toward the target and then the quantum receiver tries to collect the reflected photon (Irving, M. 2020).


Entangled-Photon Quantum Radar


Entangled-Photon QR uses the principle of Quantum Entanglement (Mathewes, M. 2010). The essence of the principle is that it is possible to link a pair of two particles regardless the distance between and form a quantum entanglement pair (Irving, M. 2020; MIT Technology Review, 2019). The production of entangled photons relies on laws of conservation of momentum and energy (IEEEXplore, 2017). Non-linear crystal is used to split photon beams into pairs of photons that have combined energy and momentum of the original photon and crystal lattice (IEEEXplore, 2017). One of the particles is fired at the target, while the second one is stored in the quantum memory of the computer (Mathewes, M. 2010). The connection between the particles is so strong that when changes happen to one of the particles the results can also be seen on the other particle (Assouly; Dassonneville;Peronnin; Bienfait; Huard, 2023). This is called the principle of Quantum entanglement or ‘Spooky action at the distance’ (Irving, M. 2020). The reflected particle is than collected by the quantum receiver and combined with the stored particle and if the object is detected then the transmitter starts producing more photons to create a detailed image of an aircraft (IEEEXplore, 2017; MIT Technology Review, 2019). If an aircraft is not identified, then the fired particle will be lost in the noisy environment (IEEEXplore, 2017).


Challenges


However, to create such a technically complicated detection system a few challenges must be resolved. The main problem is the storage of second particle, while the first one is completing its journey towards the object and back to the receiver (IEEEXplore, 2017). So, one of the options to deal with it is to design and create a quantum memory, which might be not one of the easiest tasks to complete in terms of engineering (Mathewes, M. 2010). Additionally, Quantum Memory won’t work just by itself, hence additional infrastructure such as Quantum computing system should be established (Assouly; Dassonneville; Peronnin; Bienfait; Huard, 2023).


Conclusion


Overall, Quantum radar is a very promising technology, which will have an immensely powerful impact on civil and military environments (Mathewes, M. 2010). Currently it is not as advanced as Quantum Communications or Quantum Cryptography, but it is not as complicated as Quantum Computers and Computing Systems (Mathewes, M. 2010). However, the realisation of this technology in real life might happen in the nearest future. In July of 2023 group of French researchers at Ecole Normale Supérieure de Lyon was able to develop a prototype of a quantum radar that can outperform classical radar by 20% (Fadeli, 2023). Benjamin Huard, who was part of the research team, stated that the idea of applying QS to radars was born in 2020 after they had invented an Entangled particles generator: ‘We invented a superconducting circuit in 2020 that was able, among other things, to generate entanglement, store and manipulate microwave quantum states…. demonstrating a quantum advantage in radar sensing.’ (Fadeli, 2023)
